Correlations Discovered Between Health Risks and Poverty Rates

The graph above shows the correlations between health risks or lack of healthcare coverage and poverty rates for all the states in the US. We can see that in general terms there is a linear correlation between these two factors since the states with the highest poverty rates also have the greatest percentages of non-healthcare coverage.

For instace, take a look at Louisiana, New Mexico, and Mississip on the far right of the graph plus Texas which is an outlier and the state with less health coverage. It is worth to mention that these states also have a below-average income (<$54,454), while the states situated at the far left of the graph such as New Hampshire, Maryland, and Connecticut show income levels above $60,000.

Finally, by hovering on the bubbles we can compare other variables such as Obesity and Smoke Rates. Again, states with the highest poverty rates and the least healthcare coverage show Obesity and Smoke Rates above the mean (29% and 18%, respectively) as compared to the states with better healthcare and poverty conditions. This helps us infer that availability of healthcare services for the population can of course help diminish several problems by treating them in a timely manner.
